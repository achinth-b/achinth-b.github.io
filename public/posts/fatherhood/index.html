why I don't think I'd make a good parent

October 10, 2025
parenting life decisions relationships

I was raised, and have good parents. My father is the best father in the world.

I have the greatest examples of ideal parenting from my mother and father. If I can be half as good as a parent as they collectively were; then I think any child I raise would grow to being a formidable, self-sufficient and healthy adult. So why do I think I wouldn’t make for beign a good father? I went and asked my own - and he said (paraphrased from Kannada):

‘There will come many times when you will be faced with a crossroads. One road takes you to a place where you will be happy. The other will take you to a place that is right for your child. A good parent is the one who will always choose the road that is right for their child given all variables that are in their control; even at the cost of sacrificing their own potential happiness.’

The crossroads

I don’t like to paint many things as zero-sum. But I find it difficult to hold that premise while simultaneously believing in the truth that you need to make choices in your life; and you’ve got to live by them, & consequently, die by those choices. You can’t have your cake and eat it too.

New York, Vancouver, and the math that doesn’t add up

I love living in New York City. As much as I have desired to return, I found (and still do find) Vancouver stifling in many aspects - including but not limited to: projected income floor, lucrative career opportunity networks, better ultimate, better social scenes, etc. I would love to raise a child in Williamsburg, or Park Slope, or Prospect Heights, or Fort Greene. I take a lot of pride in this city when I see 10 year olds on the subway going to and from their middle schools. I think if you put young, impressionable minds in a busy place with limitless opportunities and social interactions at every corner, they will come to understand how the world works and build their abilities of comparing and contrasting.

But it’s fucking expensive to raise a child in New York (or my hometown of Vancouver) comfortably. Forget wanting a place that’s big enough to raise a family of four in a condo (which, by the way, in a neighbourhood I want, will cost at least $1.2 million). It seems the only reliable method to secure family-driven home ownership (a la the late 1990s to the early 2000s) is to literally become housepoor.

I am someone who hates the idea of leasing and debt. I want to reasonably be in a position where I can buy a home with as little debt as possible because I hate making other people (read: private equity and the big banks) richer off of my hard-earned and priivleged dollars. I want my wealth to take care of my loved ones and my country, not some billionaire who has the ability to purchase my tranche of some securitized product derived from my credit and mortgage payments because they’re deemed as an UHNWI.

And I still want to live here because of how enriching it’s made my life! I couldn’t be more excited for what the city has in store for me in the next ten, twenty, thirty years! Reasonably so, if I want to manage raising a family, the more ideal situation would be to go to a place that’s cheaper which entails living in a place you’re not excited about living in. This is quite literally the conflict between making a choice that’s right for your children and making a choice that will make you happy. Living in New York would make me happy, but being in a position to set up my children for success given the stability that I can provide for them would entail leaving.

The tension between wanderlust and stability

I also want to live and work in many places in the world. I want to build a base both in Vancouver and in New York City, but I also want to spend my time working and living in different places in the world.

The socio-biological window is closing

I can’t speak to other individuals, but I can speak to my life before I graduated university; which I think was pretty standard for a middle-class Canadian life. I finished high school just before I turned 18, and after five years in university, started my first full-time job as soon as I turned 23. Conversely, my partner finished her degree in four years. Both of us, being born in 2000, are twenty-five years old as of today. We make good money and are are capable of making more money in the future.

Let’s ground this in some biological truths. Female fertility drops heavy after the late 20s, which reasonably leaves until the age of 32 or 33 to have a baby with little to no difficulty. Male sperm motility has also shown to have a consequentual drop.

This reasonably gives us about 7 years before the window is going to be closed.
